Chapter 1 - Introduction

What we have here is a failure to communicate!

Merriam-Webster's Collegiate Dictionary defines:

Main Entry: math·e·mat·ics
Pronunciation: "math-'ma-tiks, "ma-th&-
Function: noun plural but usually singular in construction
Date: 1581
1 : the science of numbers and their operations, interrelations, combinations, generalizations, and abstractions and of space configurations and their structure, measurement, transformations, and generalizations
2 : a branch of, operation in, or use of mathematics [the mathematics of physical chemistry]

Main Entry: new math
Function: noun
Date: 1964
: basic mathematics taught with emphasis on abstraction and the principles of set theory -- called also new mathematics

Main Entry: flash (follow link for full definition)
Function: verb, noun, adjective
Date: 13th century, 1566, circa 17th century
: more than 400 years of vector graphics experience!

Main Entry: Flash·e·mat·ics
Pronunciation: "flash-'ma-tiks, "fla-sh&-
Function: proper noun
Date: 2000
: the interface for the integration of mathematics and new math with the Flash scripting environment...who knew?

Through the use of poetic license to embellish the actual text that Merriam-Webster provides for us, the point has been made ... the Flash environment lends itself well to combine the matter-of-factness of mathematical equations with the mind-expanding creative possibilities offered to the developer by the Flash 4 interface.

This series of articles will attempt to sort out the various mathematical functions that are commonly used with ActionScript and JavaScript in Flash. We will explore the theory leading to the script, with each new chapter featuring a theme source file and it's author(s). I will attempt to interview a wide variety of developers, from seasoned veterans who lead the way with their tested methods, to the fresh-start designer who's experiments may lead to new innovations in scripting technique.

We'll also examine a Flash game that I have been working on: The Spider vs. The Fly. At present, the game remains incomplete, due to a collision detection script that relies on the timeline for a 75 cycle iteration, which is woefully slow. The plan here is to convert this script from Actionscript to Javascript:Math.methods, thus creating an instant call for collision detection.

The game has several math functions involved in it's development. The first script deals with evaluating the shortest distance on a path. This script works very nicely as is, so explore this as a completed work, where the spider chooses from the end point of one of 2-4 paths at each spiderweb intersection. There can be adjustments to this script by employing a different coordinate system calculation, and by scripting an object-oriented architecture, which we shall discuss in chapter 1.

I have left unfinished a most important aspect of this game: user parameters. This is where I would like your creative juices to flow, and devise the most efficient plan for deploying the parameters, thus creating a robust, challenging environment for the user's entertainment. With your preliminary parameter logic as a guide, follow the progression of the code through the first 4 chapters with us, dealing with the re-scripting in steps as we discuss the relevant chapter's topic. Please submit your questions, comments or solutions to me by email, and we'll create a game for each submission to compare your results. Please also note that there is a flaw in the script, and some variables are not initializing, because the timeline stalls at frame 3 (this is due to the collision detection problem, I believe).

I will be displaying pages that use math notation, indicated by the Mathreader icon at the page link. After downloading the notebook reader, you may either view the file from source or download the notebooks for later use. Please explore each link as you get to it on the page, as the information there is relevant to the text that follows.

Now for my disclaimer: I am not a math tutor. As a matter of fact, I'm learning this all right along with you, so I apologize for the errors that I'm certain to make upon our journey. All formally trained mathematicians may now collectively snicker ... or forever hold your Pi's!

Now, let's get to the math...
