Jeremy Clark interview and technique
By Misty West,
Macromedia Developer Relations Manager
Jeremy Clark is Macromedia's answer to the Flash design guru. With a repertoire that spans graphic design for print, for the early Web, Shockwave, and Flash, and a background in network administration, he's become a citizen of the deep interactive Web.

We figured the time was right to talk to Jeremy about Flash 4, and push a bit into its implications for shockwave.com.

When did you first see the Web?
I was active on the Internet before the Web existed. I used Gopher to do school research and to download weather maps from Japan, to see what the weather was like where I used to live.

You lived in Japan?
Yes, for seven years. I was a teenager. It was cool—was a good experience to grow up in a different culture, learning a different language and seeing a different approach to life. I was actually a Unix system administrator in Japan for a year, after I graduated from high school. They had a Unix network system that was all HP machines, and all the manuals were in English. They needed somebody who could read English, use the manuals, and learn to run the system.

So when did you get involved with the web?
I guess my interest in the Internet grew initially from my technical background. As soon as Mosaic came out, I was intrigued to see how I could meld that with my design interests, which I was following in school. I was a graphic design major, studying traditional print design.

So how did you bridge the gap between print and Web design?
My interest in pursuing the Web as a platform for design spawned from my frustration with the process in print design, and with prepress. When I was studying print design, I worked a lot with prepress. I did imaging, and I worked in print shops on the actual press. I did that consciously, to get an overview of the whole design process—so that when I became a designer, I would have an edge over other designers by knowing how to smooth that process.

But in the midst of that, I just saw how frustrating the whole thing was. As a designer, I felt my job was finished as soon as I had a design I was happy with in Photoshop and Quark. The appeal of the Web was that it was actually finished, almost, at that point, except for formatting the HTML.

Except for formatting the HTML?
That barrier was what led me to learn HTML. I learned most of my HTML from using the view source tool, and from early online guides. That was the beauty of the Web—that I could learn it without having to buy books or go to school for it.

Another hook for me was that before a lot of people knew about the Web—when AOL and CompuServe were becoming big companies—no one really knew what the Internet was. I felt strongly that there was a big future for this new platform and was determined to help further it's growth.

Definitely... and how did you start working in the field?
I was consulting for a real estate agent on backend systems for a while, and I convinced them that they needed a Web site. Theirs became one of the first real estate agencies on the Web. They didn't understand why they needed a Web site, but since then, they've gotten a lot of their business from their Web site, and they've thanked me over and over.

Soon after that I started getting into animation as well - I was an addict as soon as I saw the betas of Netscape 2, which allowed animated GIFs, and early Shockwave blew me away. At that point people were just making movies with their 3D logo spinning around but I knew that the living, interactive Web was something I wanted to be involved with.
